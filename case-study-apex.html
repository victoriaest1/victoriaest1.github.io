Project Overview

Bringing Formula 1 closer to its fans

APEX started as a personal passion project — I'm a big F1 fan and I kept getting frustrated during races trying to find live standings, driver data, and lap times across multiple apps. So I decided to design the one I actually wanted to use.

This was my first solo UX/UI project from scratch. No brief, no client — just me, my Figma file, and a lot of race weekend energy. I handled everything: research, user flows, visual design, and the full prototype.

The Challenge

What problem was worth solving?

During race weekends I'd have four different tabs open — live timing, driver standings, lap data, news. It was chaos. The existing apps are either too cluttered or too basic, and none of them feel built for someone who actually lives and breathes the sport.

😤
Too much, too cluttered
Existing F1 apps try to do everything at once — ads, news, shop, fantasy, live data. During a race you don't have time to search through all of that.
Pain point #1
🔍
Data buried too deep
Lap times, tyre strategies, driver gaps — the data fans actually care about is buried 3 taps deep. It should be front and centre, not hidden behind a paywall.
Pain point #2
🌑
No dark mode done right
F1 fans watch races at night, in dark rooms. A proper dark-first design isn't a nice-to-have — it's essential for the experience to feel truly immersive.
Pain point #3
The Solution

Speed, clarity, and immersion

APEX is built around one idea: get the right information to the fan as fast as possible, without noise. Every screen decision came from asking "would I actually use this during a race?" If the answer was no, it got cut.

  • 🏎️
    Real-time race experience — Live standings, gaps, lap times and tyre data all visible on one screen. No scrolling required when the lights go out.
  • 📊
    Clear data hierarchy — The most important info (position, gap to leader, pit status) is always the biggest on screen. Supporting data sits below without competing for attention.
  • 🗺️
    Intuitive navigation — Five tabs, each with one clear purpose: Race, Drivers, Teams, Schedule, and News. You always know where you are.
  • 🌙
    Dark-first design — Designed entirely in dark mode with team colour accents. Easy on the eyes whether you're watching at midnight or in a packed fan zone.
Design Process

From research to prototype

I followed a full design process — even as a solo project. Skipping steps felt like a shortcut I'd regret, so I made sure to do the research properly before touching Figma.

Delivery
1
Research & Discovery
I audited the F1 app, ESPN F1, and third-party tools. I also talked to fellow fans — what screens did they have open during a race? What annoyed them? The answers were consistent and very clear.
2
Define & Synthesise
Mapped out the key user journeys: checking live standings, exploring a driver's season stats, and following a race from start to finish. These became the backbone of the 5 user flows.
3
Ideation & Wireframes
Started with rough sketches on paper, then moved to low-fidelity wireframes in Figma. Focus was on information hierarchy — getting the right data visible at a glance before worrying about how it looked.
4
High-Fidelity Prototype
Built all 23 screens in Figma with a full dark-mode design system — components, colour tokens, type styles. Everything connected into a working prototype you can actually tap through.
5
Usability Testing
Shared the prototype with F1 fans from my circle and watched them navigate it. A few flows needed rethinking — especially the live race view, where I had too much data competing for attention.
6
Iteration & Polish
After feedback, I simplified the live race screen, improved tap target sizes, and added micro-interactions. The final version is cleaner and faster to navigate than what I started with.

Reflections

What I learned building APEX

🏎️
Speed matters in UX too
Being a fan is a design superpower. I already knew what was broken about the existing apps — that passion drove more opinionated, more honest design decisions than any brief could.
📐
Design systems save you
Building a proper component library early meant that when I redesigned screens after testing, I only had to update one component — not 23 screens one by one.
🔁
Test before you polish
I got attached to my live race screen and spent too long perfecting it — only to simplify it after feedback. Ship the rough version to real users first, always.
🌑
Dark mode needs its own system
You can't just invert a light design. Dark-first UI needs its own thinking around contrast, depth, and how colours behave on dark backgrounds.
🎯
Hierarchy is everything
F1 data is complex — laps, gaps, tyres, safety cars. The hardest part wasn't adding information, it was deciding what NOT to show on each screen.
✨
Details make you feel it
The team colour highlights, the tyre compound icons, the micro-interactions — none of these are strictly necessary. But they're what make the app feel alive and worth using.
Next Steps

APEX started as a personal exercise but it's become one of my favourite things I've made. I'd love to push it further — adding a fantasy league module, a race replay timeline, and driver head-to-head comparison screens.

For now, the prototype is live and fully tappable. Give it a go before the next race weekend. 🏎️
